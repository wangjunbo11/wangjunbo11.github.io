## 📌 Background

Export duration is a key mechanism for accumulating experiential knowledge and facilitating learning in international trade. Using HS 6-digit level Chinese agricultural export data, this study integrates export duration and new market entry into a unified analytical framework to examine both the impact and underlying mechanisms of export learning in agriculture.

---

## 📊 Main Findings

The empirical results show that export duration has a **significant inverted U-shaped effect** on new market entry.

This relationship varies across:
- Export learning environments across regions  
- Levels of agricultural product processing  
- Exposure to non-tariff trade barriers  

---

## 🔬 Mechanisms

We identify two primary channels through which export duration affects new market entry:

- **Information search cost reduction**
- **Productivity enhancement (TFP growth)**

These mechanisms jointly explain how accumulated export experience facilitates market expansion at early stages but generates diminishing returns over time.

---

## 🌍 Spillover Effects

We further find evidence of **cross-market and cross-product spillover effects**, indicating that export duration not only influences entry decisions in specific markets but also generates path dependence in firms’ subsequent export strategies.

---

## 📈 Contributions

This paper contributes to the literature in three ways:

- Extends export learning theory by introducing a nonlinear dynamic effect of export duration  
- Provides micro-level evidence on trade expansion behavior in agricultural sectors  
- Highlights the role of information frictions and productivity in shaping market entry decisions  

---

## 🧠 Policy Implications

The findings provide important insights for policymakers in developing countries by:

- Supporting export experience accumulation strategies  
- Promoting diversification of agricultural export markets  
- Designing targeted trade facilitation policies to reduce information frictions
